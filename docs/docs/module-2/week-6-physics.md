# Week 6 - Physics for Humanoid Robots

## Contact, Friction, and Force Concepts

Understanding physics is fundamental to humanoid robotics, as robots must interact with the physical world through contact, friction, and force. Unlike digital systems that operate in frictionless, weightless virtual environments, humanoid robots must navigate the complex physics of real-world interaction.

### The Nature of Contact

Contact is the fundamental mechanism through which humanoid robots interact with their environment:

#### Types of Contact
- **Point Contact**: Interaction at a single point (like a finger touching a surface)
- **Line Contact**: Interaction along a line (like a wheel rolling on a surface)
- **Surface Contact**: Interaction across an area (like a foot on the ground)
- **Multi-point Contact**: Simultaneous contact at multiple locations (like hands on a bar)

#### Contact Mechanics
When two surfaces come into contact, several physical phenomena occur:
- **Normal Forces**: Forces perpendicular to the contact surface
- **Tangential Forces**: Forces parallel to the contact surface
- **Contact Stiffness**: How much the surfaces deform under load
- **Contact Damping**: Energy dissipation during contact

### Friction: The Double-Edged Force

Friction is simultaneously essential for robot operation and a source of complexity:

#### Static Friction
- Prevents objects from sliding when force is applied
- Allows robots to maintain grip and stability
- Must be overcome to initiate motion

#### Dynamic Friction
- Occurs during sliding motion
- Generally lower than static friction
- Creates energy losses and heat

#### Rolling Friction
- Applies to wheels and rolling contacts
- Generally much lower than sliding friction
- Important for mobile robots

#### Friction Modeling Challenges
- Friction coefficients vary with materials, surfaces, and conditions
- Stribeck effect: friction changes with velocity
- Temperature and wear affect friction over time

### Force Concepts in Humanoid Robotics

Forces govern all physical interactions in humanoid systems:

#### Internal Forces
- **Joint Torques**: Forces generated by actuators to move robot limbs
- **Muscle-like Forces**: In some bio-inspired systems, forces that mimic muscle behavior
- **Structural Forces**: Internal forces that maintain robot structure

#### External Forces
- **Gravitational Forces**: Constant downward force on all masses
- **Contact Forces**: Forces from interaction with environment
- **Applied Forces**: Forces from external sources (pushes, pulls)

#### Force Control Strategies
- **Impedance Control**: Controlling the robot's mechanical impedance
- **Admittance Control**: Controlling how the robot responds to external forces
- **Hybrid Force-Position Control**: Combining position and force control

## How Humanoids Interact with Ground

Ground interaction is fundamental to humanoid locomotion and stability. Unlike wheeled robots that maintain continuous contact, bipedal humanoids must manage complex contact transitions.

### Ground Contact Mechanics

#### Support Polygon
- The area defined by ground contact points
- For bipedal robots, this changes as feet move
- The center of mass must remain within this polygon for static stability

#### Pressure Distribution
- Force is distributed across the contact surface
- Uneven distribution can cause slipping or tipping
- Important for balance and safe interaction

#### Ground Reaction Forces
- Forces exerted by the ground on the robot
- Equal and opposite to forces exerted by the robot on the ground
- Critical for balance and locomotion control

### Walking Physics

#### Single Support Phase
- Only one foot is in contact with the ground
- Robot must maintain balance with reduced support
- Center of mass must be carefully controlled

#### Double Support Phase
- Both feet are in contact with the ground
- Maximum stability but limited mobility
- Transition phase between steps

#### Swing Phase
- Non-support foot moves to next contact position
- Must clear ground while maintaining balance
- Trajectory planning is critical

### Ground Properties and Adaptation

#### Surface Stiffness
- Hard surfaces (concrete) vs. soft surfaces (grass, sand)
- Affects ground reaction forces and stability
- Requires different control strategies

#### Surface Friction
- High-friction surfaces allow more aggressive movement
- Low-friction surfaces require conservative control
- Slip detection and recovery strategies needed

#### Surface Irregularities
- Steps, slopes, and obstacles require adaptation
- Perception and planning must account for variations
- Control must handle unexpected contact changes

## Example: Pseudo Physics Scenario

Let's examine a conceptual physics scenario that demonstrates the principles of physical interaction for humanoid robots:

### Scenario: Stepping onto a Moving Surface

Consider a humanoid robot stepping onto a surface that begins moving horizontally after contact is established. This scenario illustrates several key physics concepts:

```
Initial State:
- Robot approaches stationary surface
- Single support: stance foot on ground, swing foot moving
- Center of mass positioned appropriately

Contact Phase:
- Swing foot makes contact with surface
- Ground reaction forces establish
- Robot begins to sense surface properties

Surface Motion:
- Surface begins moving horizontally
- Friction forces act on contact foot
- Robot must respond to maintain balance

Response Strategies:
- Ankle adjustment to maintain balance
- Hip movement to shift center of mass
- Potential stepping strategy to expand support
```

### Physics Analysis

#### Force Analysis
```
∑F_horizontal = m * a_horizontal
∑F_vertical = N - mg = m * a_vertical

Where:
- N = Normal force from ground
- m = Robot mass
- g = Gravitational acceleration
- a = Acceleration components
```

#### Moment Analysis
```
∑M = I * α

Where:
- M = Moments about center of mass
- I = Moment of inertia
- α = Angular acceleration
```

### Control Response

The robot must respond to this scenario using physics-based control:

#### Immediate Response (0-100ms)
- Detect unexpected horizontal force through sensors
- Adjust ankle stiffness to resist motion
- Begin repositioning center of mass

#### Short-term Response (100ms-1s)
- Evaluate stability margins
- Plan potential stepping action
- Adjust overall body posture

#### Long-term Response (1s+)
- Implement appropriate strategy (ride with motion, step to new position, etc.)
- Return to stable configuration
- Update internal models of surface properties

### Key Physics Principles Demonstrated

This scenario illustrates several important physics concepts:

#### Conservation of Momentum
- The robot's momentum changes due to external forces
- Control must account for momentum transfer

#### Friction Limitations
- Available friction determines maximum sustainable force
- Control must respect friction constraints

#### Dynamic Balance
- Balance is maintained through active control, not passive stability
- Continuous adjustment is required

#### Impulse and Impact
- Contact creates impulsive forces that must be managed
- Control must handle impact without damage

## Physics Simulation and Modeling

### Simulation Approaches

#### Rigid Body Simulation
- Models objects as perfectly rigid bodies
- Efficient but may not capture important deformation effects
- Good for basic contact and collision

#### Soft Body Simulation
- Models object deformation under force
- More realistic but computationally expensive
- Important for safe human interaction

#### Hybrid Approaches
- Combines rigid and soft body models
- Optimizes for specific applications
- Balances accuracy with computational efficiency

### Physics Engines

Common physics engines used in humanoid robotics:

#### Bullet Physics
- Fast, robust collision detection
- Good for real-time applications
- Widely used in robotics simulation

#### ODE (Open Dynamics Engine)
- Specialized for robotics applications
- Good for articulated body simulation
- Supports various joint types

#### MuJoCo
- High-fidelity physics simulation
- Excellent for research applications
- Supports complex contact models

## Physics-Based Control Strategies

### Impedance Control
- Controls the robot's mechanical impedance (stiffness, damping)
- Allows compliant interaction with environment
- Essential for safe human-robot interaction

### Hybrid Force-Position Control
- Combines position control with force control
- Useful for contact-rich tasks
- Allows precise positioning while managing contact forces

### Passivity-Based Control
- Ensures energy conservation in control systems
- Provides stability guarantees
- Important for safe interaction
